## 1. Background and Objectives

In recent years, the widespread use of car navigation systems and smartphone map applications has enabled drivers to instantly obtain routes with the shortest travel time. However, although such route choices are efficient from an individual perspective, they are not necessarily desirable from a societal perspective. For example, traffic congestion not only increases travel time but also increases CO₂ emissions through higher fuel consumption. Furthermore, an increase in through traffic in residential areas leads to greater noise exposure and increases the risk of traffic accidents involving pedestrians and vehicles. In this way, individual travel behavior imposes various impacts on surrounding people and the environment. These impacts are referred to as **negative externalities**, many of which are not directly borne by road users themselves.

In the field of transportation engineering, this issue has traditionally been explained based on the two principles proposed by Wardrop. User Equilibrium (UE) is defined as a state in which all used routes have equal and minimal travel times, while unused routes have equal or longer travel times. In contrast, System Optimum (SO) is defined as the traffic assignment that minimizes the total travel time over the entire road network. Previous studies, including those summarized by the Japan Society of Civil Engineers (JSCE), have mainly focused on system-optimal traffic assignment considering congestion effects. Although congestion, CO₂ emissions, noise, and traffic accident risk have each been evaluated individually, only a limited number of studies have incorporated these externalities simultaneously into a unified traffic assignment framework.

Meanwhile, the increasing availability of Controller Area Network (CAN) data has made it possible to develop route guidance strategies that reflect vehicle-specific driving characteristics and operating conditions.

Therefore, this study develops a traffic assignment model that evaluates multiple externalities within a unified framework. The objectives of this study are:

1. To compare the relative impacts of different types of externalities.
2. To clarify how considering one externality influences the others.

## 2. Methodology

### 2.1 Formulation of Externalities

In this study, four types of traffic externalities—congestion, CO₂ emissions, noise, and traffic accidents—were formulated at the road link level.

#### (a) Private Cost and Congestion Externality

The private travel cost is represented by the Bureau of Public Roads (BPR) function:

$$
t_a(x_a)=t_{0,a}\left(1+\alpha\left(\frac{x_a}{c_a}\right)^\beta\right)
\qquad (1)
$$

where $x_a$ denotes the traffic flow on link $a$, $c_a$ is the link capacity, and $t_{0,a}$ is the free-flow travel time.

The congestion externality is defined as the additional travel time imposed on other road users due to an increase in traffic volume and is expressed as the marginal social cost:

$$
C_a^{ex}(x_a)=x_a\frac{dt_a(x_a)}{dx_a}
\qquad (2)
$$

#### (b) CO₂ Emission Cost

The CO₂ emission cost is calculated using the speed-dependent emission factor proposed by Barth et al.:

$$
\frac{p_{CO_2}}{\mathrm{VOT}}
\cdot
\frac{EF(v)\cdot L_a}{1000}
\qquad (3)
$$

where $p_{CO_2}$ is the carbon price, VOT denotes the value of travel time, $L_a$ is the length of link $a$, and the unit of $C_a^{CO_2}$ is minutes per vehicle.

#### (c) Noise Cost

Noise exposure is evaluated using a distance-decay model based on the gravity model framework proposed by Sheppard. The exposure score for each road link is calculated as

$$
S_a=\sum_i N_i\exp(-\beta d_{ai})
\qquad (4)
$$

where $N_i$ represents the residential scale of building $i$, and $d_{ai}$ is the distance between link $a$ and building $i$.

The corresponding noise cost is expressed as

$$
\frac{u_{noise}}{\mathrm{VOT}}
\cdot
L_a
\left(
\frac{S_a}{\bar{S}}
\right)
\qquad (5)
$$

where $u_{noise}$ is the unit noise cost per unit distance and $\bar{S}$ denotes the average exposure score. The unit of $C_a^{noise}$ is minutes per vehicle.

#### (d) Accident Cost

The accident cost is formulated based on the expected accident loss per unit travel distance proposed by Dai et al.:

$$
\frac{M_DD_a+M_II_a}
{\mathrm{VOT}\cdot Q_a^{UE}}
\qquad (6)
$$

where $M_D$ and $M_I$ denote the economic losses per fatality and per injury, respectively; $D_a$ and $I_a$ represent the numbers of fatalities and injuries associated with link $a$; and $Q_a^{UE}$ is the link traffic volume obtained under User Equilibrium (UE). The unit of $C_a^{acc}$ is minutes per vehicle.

#### (e) Generalized Cost

The generalized travel cost is defined by integrating all externality costs as

$$
t_a(x_a)
+
C_a^{cong}(x_a)
+
C_a^{CO_2}(x_a)
+
C_a^{noise}(x_a)
+
C_a^{acc}(x_a)
\qquad (7)
$$

Traffic assignment was performed for all possible combinations of the four externalities, resulting in a total of sixteen assignment scenarios, including the User Equilibrium (UE) case.

### 2.2 Analysis Network

The **Sioux Falls network**, which is widely used as a benchmark network in traffic assignment studies, was adopted for theoretical validation, as shown in **Figure 1**. The network consists of **24 nodes**, **76 directed links**, **528 origin–destination (OD) pairs**, and a total demand of **360,600 trips**.

### 2.3 Traffic Assignment Method

A **Stochastic User Equilibrium (SUE)** model was employed because it can represent drivers' imperfect information and perception errors in route choice. By setting the dispersion parameter $\mu$ sufficiently large, the SUE solution closely approximates the **User Equilibrium (UE)** solution.

Route choice probabilities were modeled using a **Logit-based route choice model**, and traffic flows were updated using the **Method of Successive Averages (MSA)** until the convergence criterion was satisfied.
